History

House Sparrows historically lived in Europe, but during the colonization of America were brought to America in an attempt to control an outbreak of linden moths in New York City in 1852. However, they rapidly spread out of control, and have spread across the entirety of the United States. They have similarly spread and adapted to many other areas across the world due to being brought by ships. In fact, a study has been done which utilized the sounds of their chirrups in radio broadcasts across the world to determine their range.

General Appearance and Anatomy

House Sparrows are small and brown, with speckles of chestnut, grey, white and black creating a brownish overall plumage. They have smallish, pointed wings suited for flying shorter distances in trees or in bushes. These wings have a good balance of breadth to allow for a lot of lift to allow quick takeoff and pointed wingtips allowing for good maneuvering. Along with these compact yet versatile wings, they sport a long, mobile tail that allows for a wide range of steering. Their flight is direct and with no gliding. Males have a small black throat patch and grey patch on the top of their head, while females lack those distinguishing features and are the 'base' House Sparrow, as fledgling and immature members of the species share the same appearance.

Fun Facts

  • They are one of three species not protected federally, along with the European Starling and Rock Pigeion. This is due to them being invasive species.
  • They can swim if needs must, though are never observed swimming for any purpose other than to escape water.
  • They are able to live in many environments, from snowy New York streets to the arid Sonoran Desert.
  • They can live up to 13 years.
